Common Furnace Problems in Colorado Springs

local_fire_department

Ignitor Failure

The most common furnace repair in Colorado Springs. Hot surface ignitors crack from repeated thermal cycling — especially during chinook events when temperatures swing 40°F in hours, causing the furnace to cycle on and off rapidly.

Symptoms: Furnace clicks but no flame, blower runs but no heat, repeated lockout codes

sensors

Flame Sensor Fouling

At 6,035 ft elevation, lower oxygen density causes incomplete combustion that deposits carbon residue on the flame sensor faster than at sea level. A fouled sensor cannot detect the flame and shuts the burner down within seconds of ignition.

Symptoms: Furnace lights then shuts off after 3–5 seconds, yellow or orange flame, repeated restart attempts

air

Blower Motor Failure

Blower motors push heated air through your ductwork. At altitude, lower air density means the motor works harder to move the same volume of conditioned air. Bearings wear, capacitors weaken, and ECM motors develop control board faults over time.

Symptoms: Weak airflow, loud humming or squealing, furnace overheating and tripping high-limit switch

warning

Heat Exchanger Cracks

A cracked heat exchanger is a carbon monoxide hazard and the most serious furnace problem we diagnose. Repeated thermal expansion and contraction — amplified by Colorado Springs' extreme temperature swings — causes metal fatigue and hairline cracks that leak combustion gases.

Symptoms: CO detector alarms, soot around burner area, visible flame rollout, strong aldehyde smell

mode_fan

Draft Inducer Motor

The draft inducer creates negative pressure to vent combustion gases safely. At altitude, lower atmospheric pressure means the inducer must generate proportionally more draft. Failed bearings and cracked housings are common in units over 10 years old.

Symptoms: Loud rattling before ignition, pressure switch error codes, furnace refuses to light

toggle_on

Pressure Switch Errors

Pressure switches verify proper venting before allowing ignition. Colorado Springs' lower atmospheric pressure (approximately 24.5 inHg vs 29.9 inHg at sea level) causes false negatives in switches calibrated for lower elevations. Clogged condensate drains and blocked vent pipes compound the problem.

Symptoms: Furnace starts draft inducer but never ignites, pressure switch error code, intermittent lockouts

High-Altitude HVAC Science

Altitude & Furnace Performance at 6,000+ Feet

Colorado Springs sits at 6,035 ft above sea level — and altitude fundamentally changes how your furnace operates. The industry standard derating formula reduces furnace output by approximately 4% for every 1,000 feet above sea level, which means a furnace rated at 80,000 BTU at sea level delivers only about 61,000 BTU in Colorado Springs — a 24% loss in heating capacity.

This derating happens because combustion requires oxygen. At 6,035 ft, atmospheric pressure drops to roughly 24.5 inHg (compared to 29.9 inHg at sea level), which means 18% less oxygen is available per cubic foot of air. Your furnace burners receive less oxygen, produce less heat per therm of gas, and generate more incomplete combustion byproducts including carbon monoxide.

This is why proper altitude derating and Manual J load calculations are critical for every furnace installation and repair in Colorado Springs. An oversized furnace short-cycles, wastes fuel, and accelerates wear. An undersized unit runs continuously during sub-zero nights and cannot maintain setpoint temperature. Both scenarios lead to premature component failure and higher repair costs.

Altitude Effects on Combustion

  • air 18% less oxygen density — incomplete combustion increases carbon residue on flame sensors
  • thermostat Lower atmospheric pressure — pressure switches and draft inducers require altitude calibration
  • dangerous Higher CO risk — incomplete combustion at altitude produces more carbon monoxide per therm burned

Carbon Monoxide Warning

Furnaces operating at altitude produce more carbon monoxide due to incomplete combustion. A cracked heat exchanger at 6,035 ft is more dangerous than at sea level because CO concentrations in flue gases are higher. If your CO detector alarms, evacuate immediately and call 911, then call (719) 304-5003 for emergency furnace inspection.

Furnace Repair FAQ — Colorado Springs

How much does furnace repair cost in Colorado Springs?

Most furnace repairs in Colorado Springs cost $150–$650 depending on the component. Ignitor replacement runs $150–$300, flame sensor cleaning $89–$150, blower motor replacement $350–$650, and heat exchanger repair $500–$1,500. Colorado Springs Elite HVAC charges an $89 diagnostic fee that is waived when you approve the repair — you get a flat-rate written quote before any work begins.

What are the most common furnace problems at altitude?

At 6,035 ft in Colorado Springs, the most common furnace problems are ignitor failure from thermal stress caused by rapid temperature swings, flame sensor fouling due to incomplete combustion in thin air, pressure switch errors from lower atmospheric pressure, and short-cycling caused by oversized units that were not properly derated for altitude. Furnaces lose approximately 4% efficiency per 1,000 ft above sea level — about 24% total derating in Colorado Springs.

How do I know if my furnace needs repair or replacement?

Replace your furnace if it is over 15–20 years old, requires a repair costing more than 50% of a new unit, has a cracked heat exchanger, or operates below 80% AFUE efficiency. Repair is the better option if the unit is under 12 years old, the repair cost is under $500, and the system still heats your home evenly. Our NATE-certified technicians provide honest assessments — we never push replacements when a repair will do.

Why does my furnace short-cycle in Colorado Springs?

Furnace short-cycling in Colorado Springs is commonly caused by an oversized unit that was not derated for 6,035 ft altitude, a dirty flame sensor shutting down the burner prematurely, a clogged air filter restricting airflow and triggering the high-limit switch, or a faulty pressure switch reacting to lower atmospheric pressure at elevation. A proper diagnostic identifies the root cause — short-cycling wastes fuel and accelerates wear on the heat exchanger.
