Altitude-Calibrated Equipment

High-Altitude Furnace Installation in Monument

Standard furnace installations assume near-sea-level oxygen density. At Monument's 7,100+ feet, that assumption breaks down. Gas burner orifices sized for lower altitudes deliver too much fuel relative to the available oxygen, producing incomplete combustion, elevated carbon monoxide output, and flame rollout risk. Pressure switches calibrated for sea-level barometric pressure may fail to prove draft at altitude, causing nuisance lockouts on cold nights when you need heat most.

Colorado Springs Elite HVAC installs every Monument furnace with factory high-altitude conversion kits and performs on-site combustion analysis to verify proper gas-to-air ratios. We size equipment using Manual J load calculations with altitude correction factors specific to Monument's elevation — not the generic Colorado Springs derating that many contractors apply across the board. The difference matters: a furnace properly derated for 7,100 feet runs quieter, lasts longer, and produces measurably lower CO levels than one sized for 6,035 feet.

We install Trane, Carrier, Lennox, Rheem, Goodman, and Mitsubishi equipment — selecting the right brand and model based on your home's size, insulation profile, and ductwork condition. Every installation includes a PPRBD permit, post-installation inspection, and manufacturer warranty registration.

Fuel-Source Conversion

Propane-to-Natural-Gas Conversions for Tri-Lakes Homes

Many Monument homes — particularly in older sections of Woodmoor, along the Palmer Lake corridor, and in rural areas east of I-25 — were built before natural gas infrastructure reached the Tri-Lakes area. These homes still run on propane, which costs significantly more per BTU than natural gas and requires tank maintenance, delivery scheduling, and outdoor storage that complicates landscaping and HOA compliance.

As natural gas lines extend further into the Monument area, propane-to-natural-gas conversion has become one of our most requested services. The conversion involves more than swapping orifices: we replace gas regulators, modify supply line sizing to account for the lower pressure of natural gas delivery, recalibrate all gas appliances (furnace, water heater, range, dryer), and coordinate PPRBD inspections for the new gas line connections. For homes at Monument's altitude, we also verify that the conversion maintains proper derating — natural gas and propane have different BTU-per-cubic-foot ratings that must be accounted for at elevation.

Elevation Matters

Why Monument Homes Need Altitude-Calibrated HVAC

Monument's position on the Palmer Divide creates a microclimate that differs meaningfully from Colorado Springs just 15 miles south. Winters are longer, temperatures drop lower, and wind exposure across the open terrain between Monument and Palmer Lake accelerates heat loss through walls, windows, and rooflines. Homes in elevated subdivisions like King Deer and Sanctuary Pointe face even greater exposure due to their ridgeline positions.

Beyond temperature, Monument homeowners face two issues that lower-elevation communities rarely encounter. First, well-water scaling: many Tri-Lakes homes rely on well water with high mineral content that causes calcium buildup in hydronic heating systems, boiler heat exchangers, and humidifier pads — reducing efficiency and shortening equipment life. Second, wildfire smoke: pine beetle kill areas in the surrounding Black Forest and Pike National Forest create seasonal wildfire smoke events that overwhelm standard furnace filters and demand upgraded MERV-rated filtration or dedicated air purification systems to maintain indoor air quality.

Colorado Springs Elite HVAC addresses all of these Monument-specific factors during every installation and maintenance visit. We are not a one-size-fits-all contractor — we calibrate every system to the specific demands of Tri-Lakes living.
