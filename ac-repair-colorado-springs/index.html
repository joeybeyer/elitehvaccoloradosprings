Common AC Problems in Colorado Springs

sync_problem

Short-Cycling

Your AC turns on and off every few minutes without completing a cooling cycle. Common causes include an oversized unit, dirty condenser coils, low refrigerant charge, or a failing compressor. At altitude, lower air density amplifies short-cycling in systems that were not properly sized with Manual J calculations.

ac_unit

Frozen Evaporator Coils

Ice buildup on the indoor coil blocks airflow and kills cooling output. Restricted airflow from dirty filters, a weak blower motor, or closed supply registers is the most common trigger. Refrigerant leaks also cause freeze-ups by dropping suction pressure below the dew point. Colorado Springs' dry air and rapid nighttime temperature drops increase freeze risk.

water_drop

Refrigerant Leaks

A refrigerant leak causes warm air from the vents, frozen coils, and elevated electric bills. Vibration from hailstorm impacts, UV degradation of line-set insulation, and thermal expansion stress at altitude all contribute to joint and coil leaks. We use electronic leak detectors and nitrogen pressure tests to pinpoint the exact location before quoting a repair.

bolt

Capacitor & Contactor Failure

The capacitor stores the charge that starts your compressor and fan motors. When it fails, the outdoor unit hums but doesn't spin or clicks and shuts off. Contactors — the relay switches that send power — pit and burn over time. Colorado Springs' frequent electrical storms accelerate capacitor and contactor wear. Replacement takes under an hour.

mode_fan

Condenser Fan Motor

The condenser fan pulls air through the outdoor coil to reject heat. When the motor fails, head pressure spikes and the compressor overheats and shuts down on safety. Cottonwood seeds, dust, and hail debris clog the motor bearings. We stock universal and OEM condenser fan motors for same-day replacement on most brands.

error

Compressor Lockout

A locked-out compressor is the most serious AC failure. Causes include electrical overload, internal mechanical seizure, loss of lubrication from chronic low-charge operation, or liquid slugging from a failed metering device. We test windings, start components, and refrigerant charge before recommending compressor replacement vs. full system replacement based on the unit's age and SEER rating.

Common Questions

AC Repair FAQ — Colorado Springs

How much does AC repair cost in Colorado Springs?

Most AC repairs in Colorado Springs cost $185–$650 depending on the component. Diagnostic fees run $89–$129 and are waived when you approve the repair. Capacitor replacements start at $185, refrigerant recharges at $250, and compressor replacements range $1,200–$2,500. Colorado Springs Elite HVAC provides flat-rate written quotes before any work begins.

Why does my AC freeze up in Colorado Springs?

Frozen evaporator coils in Colorado Springs typically result from restricted airflow (dirty filter or blocked return), low refrigerant charge from a leak, or a failing blower motor. The high-desert climate and dramatic temperature swings — especially cool nights dropping below 60°F while the system runs — accelerate freeze-ups. Turn the system off, set the fan to ON to thaw the coil, and call (719) 304-5003 for same-day diagnosis.

How long does an AC repair take?

Most AC repairs are completed in 1–3 hours on the same visit. Capacitor and contactor replacements take under an hour. Refrigerant leak detection and recharge typically take 1–2 hours. Compressor or condenser fan motor replacements may take 2–4 hours depending on equipment access. Our trucks carry the most common parts to avoid second trips.

Do you repair all AC brands?

Yes. Our NATE-certified technicians service all major air conditioning brands including Trane, Carrier, Lennox, Rheem, Goodman, York, Amana, Daikin, and Mitsubishi ductless systems. We stock universal and OEM parts for the most common models and can source specialty components with next-day availability.

What's the difference between R-22 and R-410A refrigerant?

R-22 (Freon) was phased out of production in 2020 due to ozone depletion. Remaining R-22 stock is scarce and expensive — $150–$300 per pound in Colorado Springs. R-410A (Puron) is the current standard, operates at higher pressures, and is more efficient. If your system uses R-22 and develops a refrigerant leak, replacing the entire unit with an R-410A system is usually more cost-effective than repeated recharges.

Should I repair or replace my air conditioner?

Use the 50% rule: if the repair cost exceeds 50% of a new system's price and the unit is over 10 years old, replacement is the better investment. Units older than 15 years, systems using R-22 refrigerant, and air conditioners with a SEER rating below 13 are strong candidates for replacement. A new 16+ SEER unit can cut cooling costs 30–50% and handles Colorado Springs' altitude and UV exposure more reliably.
