1970s Home Upgrades

Furnace Replacement for Fountain Mesa Homes

A significant portion of Fountain's housing stock — particularly in the Fountain Mesa and Heritage neighborhoods — dates to the early-to-mid 1970s. Many of these homes still run original gravity-fed furnaces or early forced-air units that were never designed for modern efficiency standards. At Fountain's 5,545-foot elevation, altitude derating means these aging systems operate well below their rated capacity, burning more gas while producing less heat.

Replacing a 1970s furnace in Fountain is not a drop-in job. Original ductwork is often undersized by current Manual D standards, return-air plenums need reconfiguration, and flue venting may require conversion from atmospheric draft to direct-vent or sealed combustion — especially critical in homes located within the Fountain Creek flood-plain where moisture intrusion and combustion air quality demand careful engineering. Colorado Springs Elite HVAC handles these full ductwork retrofits with PPRBD-permitted installations, proper Manual J load calculations, and equipment sized specifically for Fountain's altitude and climate profile.

Code Compliance

PPRBD-Permitted HVAC Work in Fountain

Fountain falls under the jurisdiction of the Pikes Peak Regional Building Department (PPRBD) for all mechanical permits. Every furnace installation, AC replacement, and major system modification performed by Colorado Springs Elite HVAC includes a PPRBD permit pull and post-installation inspection. Permitted work protects your manufacturer warranty, ensures code compliance, and preserves your home's resale value — three things that unlicensed contractors cannot guarantee.

Our team handles the entire permit process: application filing, scheduling inspections, and resolving any code questions directly with PPRBD inspectors. For 1970s Fountain homes requiring ductwork retrofits or flue conversions, permitted work is especially critical — these modifications change the combustion and ventilation profile of the entire home and must meet current International Mechanical Code standards enforced by PPRBD.
